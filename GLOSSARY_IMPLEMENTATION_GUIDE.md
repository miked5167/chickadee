# What is AAA Hockey?

## Quick Definition
AAA (Triple-A) hockey is the highest level of competitive youth hockey in North America for players typically aged 7-18.

## Detailed Explanation
AAA hockey represents the most elite tier of amateur hockey below junior and professional levels. Players compete at the AAA level to develop their skills against the best competition, gain exposure to junior and college scouts, and advance their hockey careers.

Unlike house league or recreational hockey, AAA teams require tryouts, maintain rigorous practice schedules, and travel extensively for games and tournaments. The level of play is significantly more competitive, with players demonstrating advanced skating, stick-handling, and hockey IQ.

AAA hockey serves as a critical stepping stone for players aspiring to play junior hockey, prep school hockey, or NCAA college hockey. Many professional players developed through AAA programs before advancing to higher levels.

## Key Characteristics
- Highest skill level for youth hockey in North America
- Competitive tryout process with player evaluations
- 60-80+ games per season
- Extensive travel (often multi-state or multi-province)
- Year-round training commitments
- Most direct pathway to junior and college programs
- High time commitment (15-20+ hours per week)
- Professional coaching and development programs

## Typical Season Structure
**Regular Season:**
- September through March
- 60-80 games
- Regional and national tournaments
- Showcase events for scouts

**Off-Season:**
- Spring/summer hockey programs
- Skills development camps
- Strength and conditioning training
- Tournament and showcase participation

## Cost Range
AAA hockey requires significant financial investment:
- **Team Fees:** $8,000 - $15,000+ per season
- **Equipment:** $1,000 - $2,000 annually
- **Travel:** $2,000 - $5,000+ (hotels, flights, meals)
- **Private Coaching:** $2,000 - $5,000+ (optional)
- **Showcase Events:** $500 - $2,000+ per event

**Total Annual Cost:** $15,000 - $30,000+

*Costs vary significantly by region, program, and level of commitment.*

## Next Steps from AAA Hockey
Players in AAA hockey typically advance to:
- **Prep Schools** - Academic and athletic development
- **Junior A Hockey** - USHL, BCHL, AJHL, etc.
- **Major Junior (CHL)** - OHL, WHL, QMJHL
- **NCAA Programs** - Division I, II, or III
- **U18 AAA** - Elite 18U programs
- **USHL** - Highest level of junior hockey in the US

## Comparison to Other Levels
**AAA vs AA:**
AAA is the highest competitive level; AA is one tier below with less travel and lower cost.

**AAA vs Junior Hockey:**
AAA is for youth players (under 18); Junior hockey is for players aged 16-20 preparing for college or professional hockey.

## Regional Differences
**United States:**
- Tiered system (Tier 1, Tier 2, Tier 3)
- Tier 1 AAA is highest level
- USA Hockey sanctioned

**Canada:**
- AAA, AA, A, and House League
- AAA is highest competitive youth level
- Hockey Canada sanctioned

# How to Find a Great Hockey Advisor for NCAA Recruiting

## Introduction
Navigating NCAA recruiting is complex, with strict eligibility rules, academic requirements, and recruiting timelines that vary by division. A specialized hockey advisor can help your player get noticed by college programs, understand the process, and maximize scholarship opportunities.

## What NCAA Recruiting Advisors Do
- Create professional player profiles and highlight videos
- Connect players with college coaches at target schools
- Navigate NCAA eligibility requirements and academic planning
- Advise on appropriate competition level and timing
- Guide through commitment, scholarship, and signing process
- Provide strategic planning for recruiting timeline
- Coordinate showcase tournament participation
- Facilitate campus visits and coach meetings
- Review and negotiate scholarship offers
- Ensure compliance with NCAA rules throughout process

## What to Look For

### 1. NCAA-Specific Experience
Look for advisors who have:
- Successfully placed players at NCAA programs (ask for specific numbers)
- Deep understanding of NCAA recruiting rules and eligibility requirements
- Established relationships with college coaches across divisions
- Knowledge of academic eligibility requirements and NCAA Eligibility Center
- Experience with the entire recruiting process from first contact to signing

**Why it matters:** NCAA recruiting has specific rules, timelines, and processes that differ significantly from junior hockey or other pathways. Generic hockey advisors may not understand these nuances.

### 2. Division-Specific Expertise
NCAA Division I, II, and III have vastly different recruiting processes:

**Division I:**
- Most competitive recruiting
- Athletic scholarships available (limited to 18 per team)
- Earlier recruiting timelines
- Highest level of commitment

**Division II:**
- Athletic scholarships available (limited to 13.5 per team)
- More balanced academics and athletics
- Strong competition level

**Division III:**
- No athletic scholarships (academic and need-based aid only)
- Focus on fit and academics
- Often smaller programs with tight-knit teams

**Choose an advisor who specializes in your target division(s).**

### 3. Proven Track Record
Ask about:
- **Number of players placed:** How many players have they successfully placed at NCAA schools?
- **Which schools and divisions:** Are placements at schools similar to your targets?
- **Success rate:** What percentage of their clients achieve NCAA commitments?
- **Client references:** Can they provide references from families of recent NCAA commits?
- **Specific examples:** Can they share success stories relevant to your player's profile?

**Red flag:** Advisors who won't provide specific placement numbers or references.

### 4. Network of College Coaches
Strong NCAA advisors have established relationships with:
- Division I head coaches and assistant coaches
- Division II coaching staffs
- Division III program directors
- College hockey scouts
- NCAA conference administrators

**Why it matters:** Advisors with strong coaching networks can:
- Facilitate introductions and first contacts
- Get your player's profile in front of decision-makers
- Provide inside knowledge of program needs and fit
- Help navigate competitive recruiting situations

**Ask:** "Can you describe your relationships with coaches at [specific target schools]?"

### 5. Timing & Process Knowledge
NCAA recruiting has specific timelines that vary by division:

**Division I & II Timeline:**
- Freshman/Sophomore: Academic planning begins
- Junior Year: Official contact period begins (September 1)
- Senior Year: Commitments and signing

**Division III Timeline:**
- More flexible timing
- No signing period
- Can commit earlier in some cases

**Your advisor should guide you through:**
- When coaches can contact players (and when they can't)
- When players can take official visits
- Academic eligibility deadlines
- Eligibility Center registration process
- Appropriate timing for commitments
- National Letter of Intent signing dates

### 6. Academic Counseling Capability
Strong NCAA advisors understand:
- NCAA academic eligibility requirements (GPA, SAT/ACT scores, core courses)
- How to register with NCAA Eligibility Center
- Academic planning for college-bound student-athletes
- How academic performance affects recruiting options
- Balance between athletic and academic recruiting

**Why it matters:** Academic eligibility can make or break NCAA opportunities. Many talented players miss out because of academic issues that could have been avoided with proper planning.

### 7. Video and Profile Creation Expertise
Modern NCAA recruiting requires:
- Professional highlight videos
- Full game film
- Player profiles with stats and accomplishments
- Social media presence management

**Ask:** "Do you create highlight videos, or do I need to hire separately?"

## Red Flags to Avoid

- **Guarantees of NCAA scholarships** - No advisor can guarantee specific outcomes
- **No verifiable placement history** - They should be able to name specific players and schools
- **Pressure to commit quickly** - Good advisors give you time to make informed decisions
- **Lack of NCAA rule knowledge** - They should know current recruiting rules cold
- **No references from past clients** - Legitimate advisors have satisfied families who will speak with you
- **Unrealistic promises** - "I can get anyone into Harvard" or similar claims
- **Only focuses on Division I** - Good advisors help find the best fit, not just highest level
- **Doesn't discuss academics** - Academic eligibility is critical to NCAA recruiting
- **Vague about process** - Should be able to outline clear steps and timeline
- **Poor communication** - If they're hard to reach during hiring, it won't improve

## Cost Range

NCAA recruiting advisors typically charge:

**Full-Service Packages:**
- $2,500 - $8,000 for complete recruiting process
- Usually covers 1-2 years of support
- Includes profile creation, coach outreach, showcase coordination, and commitment guidance

**Hourly Rates:**
- $150 - $300 per hour
- Good for families who only need specific help
- Can add up quickly if extensive work needed

**Retainer Packages:**
- $500 - $1,500 per month
- Ongoing support throughout recruiting process
- Typically 6-12 month commitments

**Ala Carte Services:**
- Highlight video creation: $500 - $1,500
- Player profile development: $300 - $800
- College list development: $200 - $500
- Showcase tournament coordination: $400 - $1,000

**Payment structures:**
- Some advisors require upfront payment
- Others offer payment plans
- Be wary of "pay only if successful" offers (often include hidden fees)

## Questions to Ask Potential Advisors

### About Their Experience:
1. How many players have you placed at NCAA schools in the last 3 years?
2. Which schools and divisions were those placements?
3. Can you provide references from families whose players committed to schools similar to my target list?
4. What is your success rate for clients seeking NCAA commitments?

### About Their Process:
5. What is your process for connecting players with college coaches?
6. How do you handle academic eligibility planning?
7. How often will we communicate during the recruiting process?
8. What happens if my player doesn't receive interest from our target schools?

### About Their Network:
9. Do you have existing relationships with coaches at [list your target schools]?
10. How do you stay current with coaching changes and program needs?
11. Can you facilitate introductions to coaches at schools we're interested in?

### About Costs:
12. What is your fee structure?
13. What services are included in your package?
14. Are there any additional costs I should expect?
15. What is your refund policy if we're not satisfied?

### About Their Approach:
16. How do you evaluate which division is the right fit for my player?
17. What role do you expect us (parents/player) to play in the process?
18. How do you balance athletic goals with academic fit?

## When to Hire an Advisor

Ideal timeline for NCAA recruiting support:

### Bantam/U14 (Age 13-14):
- **Focus:** Establish relationship with advisor, begin long-term planning
- **Actions:** Academic planning, identify development areas, create initial player profile
- **Not too early:** Building awareness of the process and timeline

### U15 (Age 14-15):
- **Focus:** Academic planning intensifies, initial coach awareness
- **Actions:** Ensure academic eligibility track, begin attending appropriate showcases, start coach outreach for top prospects

### U16 (Age 15-16):
- **Focus:** Active recruiting begins, college list development
- **Actions:** Official showcase participation, direct coach contact, campus visit planning, highlight video creation
- **Critical year:** Most Division I interest surfaces during this year

### U17-18 (Age 16-18):
- **Focus:** Commitments, scholarship negotiation, final decisions
- **Actions:** Campus visits, scholarship offers, National Letter of Intent signing, final school selection
- **Make or break:** This is when most commitments happen

**Too Late?**
It's never too late to hire an advisor, but starting earlier provides more options and reduces stress. Even players in their draft year can benefit from advisor guidance for navigating offers and making final decisions.

## Content Creation Guide

### Writing Style Guidelines

**Tone:**
- Conversational but professional
- Helpful and educational
- Empathetic to families' confusion
- Confident but not arrogant
- Clear and jargon-free

**Voice:**
- Write for parents who know nothing about hockey pathways
- Assume zero prior knowledge
- Define every acronym on first use
- Use examples liberally
- Break complex concepts into simple language

**Formatting:**
- Use short paragraphs (2-4 sentences max)
- Use bullet points for scannable lists
- Use bold for key terms and important points
- Use headers frequently (every 200-300 words)
- Include visual breaks (line breaks, boxes)

---

### Example Content Do's and Don'ts

**❌ DON'T write like this:**
```
The CHL, comprising the OHL, WHL, and QMJHL, offers
elite competition for draft-eligible players pursuing
professional pathways via the NHL Entry Draft system.
```

**✅ DO write like this:**
```
The CHL (Canadian Hockey League) is made up of three
major junior leagues:
- OHL (Ontario Hockey League)
- WHL (Western Hockey League)
- QMJHL (Quebec Major Junior Hockey League)

Players in the CHL are competing at the highest level
of junior hockey in Canada. Many go on to be drafted
by NHL teams.
```

**Why the second version is better:**
- Defines all acronyms
- Uses bullet points for clarity
- Shorter sentences
- Plain language
- More accessible to parents
